Finding a vaccine and preventing a pandemic

Five soon-to-be data scientists, a deserted UAS campus and a zombie pandemic that is spreading faster than the rumours in the canteen queue - great. With more caffeine than hope and a pinch of sheer desperation, the five of them set off in search of a vaccine while the undead impatiently wait outside for their next brainy snack. Between countless CSV files, heated discussions about the ethical boundaries of substance abuse and animal testing (Do we REALLY think, that zombies are suitable test subjects?) and the desperate hunt for the last packet of instant noodles, it soon becomes clear that this is no ordinary research project.

Outbreak Analytics: When the Model Spells Doom

A virus that spreads like wildfire, an algorithm that barely keeps up, and a country map that now looks more like a doomsday simulation—fantastic. With color-coded heatmaps, outdated infection models and a growing sense of impending doom, we watched as red zones swallow familiar places. Upper Austria? Overrun. Vienna? A lost cause. Our own lab? A flickering green dot in a sea of danger—at least, for now. While we scramble to refine our projections (and argue over whether the zombies move in random walks or follow predictive patterns), one thing is certain: if our model is right, we’re running out of time.

The vital facts: How to distinguish between healthy and infected people

The data scientists in the lab sat in front of their screens - surrounded by coffee cups, empty pizza boxes and a whiteboard full of arrows, numbers and desperate notes. They hunted through endless tables of test subjects' vital signs, looking for patterns - and lo and behold, infected people not only had a significantly higher body temperature, but also alarmingly high blood pressure. ‘No wonder they're so aggressive - they're basically walking espresso machines!’ someone exclaimed, while another drew a wild curve on a graph. Between heart rate analyses and heated discussions about zombie metabolism, it slowly became clear how the virus could be specifically slowed down.

How we get the vaccine?

In the chaos of a zombie outbreak, our lab transformed into a high-stakes arena where every second counted. With little more than caffeine-fueled determination and a mountain of messy data, our team of unlikely heroes embarked on a mission: to create the vaccine that could finally put an end to the madness. Our journey began by examining each core ingredient of the cure. Tiny yet powerful yeast kickstarts fermentation, while vast culture media fuel the process. Specialized chromatography and filtration purify every drop, and the perfect amount of syringes and packaging ensure hope reaches every corner.

Vaccine production

This bar chart shows the potential number of vaccine doses that can be produced from each ingredient. Each bar represents an ingredient's capacity—calculated by comparing the available quantity with the amount required for 100 doses. The orange bar marks the bottleneck, the ingredient that limits production. In our case, "Culture media" produces the fewest doses, capping the overall output.

Distributing the vaccine

As soon as the vaccine was ready, the trucks started their engines: in a mixture of scientific precision and pure panic, the promising doses of freshly brewed healthy-juice were loaded into trucks that sped off campus with squealing tyres. With an ingenious distribution system that was was about as chaotic as a cleaning plan discussion in a shared appartment, but somehow somehow worked, they brought the saving syringe to every corner of Austria. Motorways became high-speed delivery routes, improvised drop-off points were established in the remaining safe zones and thanks to a few courageous scientists with racing ambitions, the vaccine was in circulation faster than the latest university gossip.

Needles to say: We're cured!

In the following days, the cure was distributed from the safezones to the surrounding areas. Due to the rather unfortunate fact that the cure had to be injected using comically large syringes, many people bailed and tried to flee the country but were quickly stopped by the military base of Austria´s neighbouring countries. There are still areas that are not fully cured, especially Vienna and many areas in the north of the country. Vienna is especially hard to get to because the remaining rebels use to ruins of the city (Thanks for bombing Vienna, General...) to effectively keep out unwanted visitors. But the team is certain that in the following days the last infected people will be cured. They were last seen carrying the remaining yeast to their local brewery.
